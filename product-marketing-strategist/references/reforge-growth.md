<core_concept name="The Four Fits Framework">
Brian Balfour's framework for building $100M+ companies. Growth is not about optimizing one thing — it is about aligning four interdependent fits simultaneously.

**Fit 1: Market-Product Fit**
- Understand the market and customer problem BEFORE building the solution
- Market categories: existing (competing with incumbents), resegmented (finding underserved niches), or new (creating a category)
- Key questions: How urgent is the problem? How frequently does it occur? How large is the audience? What are they doing today?
- Validation: qualitative interviews + quantitative demand signals
- Product marketing role: Define the market category, articulate the problem narrative, validate willingness to pay

**Fit 2: Product-Channel Fit**
- Products are built to fit channels. Channels do NOT mold to products.
- The product's mechanics must be compatible with the acquisition channel
- Viral products need sharing built into the core experience
- Content-driven products need SEO-friendly architecture
- Sales-driven products need demo-ability and clear ROI narratives
- Product marketing role: Ensure the product experience supports the primary acquisition channel. If the product does not fit the channel, change the product — not the channel.

**Fit 3: Channel-Model Fit**
- Your business model determines which channels are viable
- Low ARPU (< $100/yr) → Viral, SEO, UGC, product-led growth
- Medium ARPU ($100-$10K/yr) → Content marketing, paid acquisition, inside sales
- High ARPU (> $10K/yr) → Direct sales, account-based marketing, partnerships
- If your CAC exceeds what your model can support, no amount of optimization fixes it
- Product marketing role: Align messaging depth and sales enablement to the model. Self-serve needs great onboarding copy. Enterprise needs battlecards and ROI calculators.

**Fit 4: Model-Market Fit**
- Basic math: ARPU × Total Addressable Customers × Realistic Capture Rate ≥ Target Revenue
- A perfect product in a $5M market cannot become a $100M business
- Product marketing role: Define the addressable market realistically (not TAM fantasies). Identify expansion paths: upsell, cross-sell, new segments.

**Critical insight:** All four fits are interdependent. Changing one changes all. A pricing change (model) affects which channels work, which affects what the product needs to do, which affects which market you serve. Product marketers must think in systems.
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Growth Loops">
Growth loops replace the traditional funnel (AARRR) as the mental model for sustainable growth.

**Why funnels are insufficient:**
- Funnels are linear: acquire → activate → retain → monetize → refer
- They treat each stage independently and miss compounding effects
- They bias teams toward top-of-funnel acquisition over retention
- They don't model how output from one cycle feeds the next

**Growth loop structure:**
1. **Input** — A new user, piece of content, or data enters the system
2. **Action** — The user takes an action that generates value
3. **Output** — That action produces something (content, invitation, data) that attracts the next input
4. The output of one cycle becomes the input of the next

**Types of growth loops:**

*Viral loops:*
- User invites → new user joins → new user invites
- Examples: Dropbox referral, Slack workspace invites, Calendly scheduling links
- Key metric: viral coefficient (K-factor) — must be > 1 for organic viral growth

*Content loops:*
- User creates content → content is indexed/shared → new users discover → new users create content
- Examples: Pinterest pins, Stack Overflow answers, YouTube videos
- Key metric: content creation rate, SEO impressions per content piece

*Paid loops:*
- Revenue → reinvest in paid acquisition → new users → revenue
- Viable only when LTV/CAC ratio > 3:1 with reasonable payback period
- Key metric: payback period, marginal CAC vs. marginal LTV

*Sales loops:*
- Happy customer → case study / referral → new prospect → new customer → expansion
- Key metric: net revenue retention, referral rate, case study conversion

*Product-led loops:*
- User achieves value → usage generates visible output → output attracts new users
- Examples: "Made with [product]" watermarks, shared dashboards, public profiles
- Key metric: organic acquisition rate, product-qualified leads (PQLs)

**For product marketing:**
- Identify which loop(s) your product can support
- Ensure messaging reinforces the loop (e.g., if viral, make sharing feel valuable not spammy)
- Build the loop into the product experience, not just the marketing campaign
- Measure loop efficiency, not just funnel conversion rates
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Product Strategy Stack">
How strategy cascades from company level to team level:

**1. Company mission** — Why we exist
**2. Company strategy** — How we win in the market
**3. Product strategy** — Which products, for whom, in what order
**4. Product roadmap** — What problems are we solving now vs. next vs. later
**5. Product goals** — How we measure success at the team level

**Each level constrains the one below it.** A team's sprint work should trace back to the company mission through this stack. If it cannot, the work is not strategically aligned.

**For product marketing:**
- Positioning should reflect the product strategy (who are we for NOW, not eventually)
- Messaging hierarchy should mirror the strategy stack: company narrative → product narrative → feature narrative
- Every piece of content should ladder up to the product strategy
- If you cannot explain why a campaign exists in terms of the strategy stack, kill it
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Retention and Engagement Framework">
Reforge's framework for understanding and improving retention:

**Retention types:**
1. **User retention** — Are users coming back? (DAU/MAU, cohort curves)
2. **Revenue retention** — Is revenue from existing customers growing? (Net Revenue Retention)
3. **Feature retention** — Are users engaging with specific features over time?

**The retention curve:**
- Plot % of users still active by cohort over time
- If the curve flattens, you have product-market fit for that segment
- If the curve goes to zero, you have a leaky bucket — no amount of acquisition fixes this

**Engagement framework:**
- **Core action**: The single action that defines an engaged user
- **Natural frequency**: How often a user should naturally do the core action
- **Engagement loops**: Triggers that bring users back (notifications, emails, habits, content updates)

**For product marketing:**
- Acquisition marketing without retention is a waste of money
- Product marketing should own the activation and early retention narrative
- Messaging should reinforce the core action ("This is what success looks like in our product")
- Lifecycle marketing should be designed around natural frequency, not arbitrary email cadences
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Monetization Framework">
How products capture value:

**Pricing axes:**
- Value metric: What unit do you charge for? (users, transactions, usage, features)
- The best value metric scales with the customer's success using the product
- Pricing tiers should map to distinct customer segments with distinct needs

**Packaging principles:**
- Good: Feature differentiation by tier that reflects usage maturity
- Better: Usage-based pricing that aligns cost with value received
- Best: Hybrid models that combine predictable base + usage upside

**For product marketing:**
- Pricing page is one of your most important conversion tools — treat it as such
- Each tier needs its own positioning: who is it for and why
- Communicate value in the customer's language, not your feature list
- Anchor pricing to the alternative (what would they pay without you?), not to your cost
</core_concept>
