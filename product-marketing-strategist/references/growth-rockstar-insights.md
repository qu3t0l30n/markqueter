<core_concept name="Go-to-Market as a System">
Go-to-market is not a launch event. It is a system that connects product value to market demand continuously.

**GTM components:**
1. **Target definition** — Who specifically are you selling to? (Not demographics — jobs to be done, pain intensity, buying triggers)
2. **Value articulation** — What transformation do you enable? (Before state → After state)
3. **Channel selection** — Where do these people already spend time and attention?
4. **Traction mechanism** — What is the repeatable, scalable motion that generates demand?
5. **Feedback loop** — How do you learn from the market and adjust in real time?

**GTM motions:**
- **Product-led**: Free trial or freemium → self-serve onboarding → upgrade triggers → expansion
- **Sales-led**: Outbound/inbound → demo → negotiation → close → expansion
- **Community-led**: Educate → build trust → create belonging → monetize access or tools
- **Content-led**: Attract with knowledge → capture with tools/templates → convert with product
- **Partner-led**: Leverage existing distribution → co-sell or co-market → revenue share

Most successful companies combine 2-3 motions, with one as the primary engine.
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Churn Measurement and Diagnostics">
Churn is the silent killer of growth. You must understand WHERE users churn, not just THAT they churn.

**Churn taxonomy:**
1. **Early churn** (Day 0-7): User never activated. Problem: onboarding, time-to-value, expectation mismatch
2. **Mid-term churn** (Day 7-30): User activated but did not form a habit. Problem: engagement loop is weak
3. **Late churn** (Day 30+): User was active but stopped. Problem: diminishing value, competitor switch, life change
4. **Revenue churn**: User downgrades or cancels paid plan. Problem: pricing-value mismatch, economic pressure

**Diagnostic questions for each churn type:**
- Early: "Did the user experience the core value? How quickly? What blocked them?"
- Mid-term: "Did the user find a reason to come back? Was there a trigger? Did they build a habit?"
- Late: "Did the product keep growing with the user? Did a competitor offer something better?"
- Revenue: "Is the user still getting value? Has their willingness to pay changed? Is the pricing model right?"

**For product marketing:**
- Early churn is often a messaging/positioning problem — you attracted the wrong user or set wrong expectations
- Mid-term churn is often an onboarding/education problem — users do not know how to get ongoing value
- Late churn is a product/positioning problem — the product is not evolving with the user's needs
- Marketing should own churn analysis alongside product, not just acquisition metrics
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Virality and Network Effects">
Not all growth is viral, and not all viral growth is the same.

**Types of virality:**
1. **Inherent virality**: The product requires others to be useful (Slack, Zoom, multiplayer games)
2. **Collaborative virality**: The product is better with others (Google Docs, Figma, Notion)
3. **Communicative virality**: Using the product generates content others see (Calendly links, Loom videos, Canva designs)
4. **Incentivized virality**: Users get a reward for inviting others (Dropbox storage, Uber credits)
5. **Word-of-mouth**: Users love the product and tell people (no mechanics, pure delight)

**Viral coefficient (K-factor):**
- K = (invites per user) × (conversion rate per invite)
- K > 1: Viral growth (each user brings more than one new user)
- K = 0.5-1: Viral amplification (not self-sustaining but multiplies other channels)
- K < 0.5: Not meaningfully viral

**Network effects vs. virality:**
- Virality = how fast the network grows
- Network effects = how the product becomes more valuable as the network grows
- You can have virality without network effects (viral video) and network effects without virality (slow-growing marketplace)

**For product marketing:**
- Identify which type of virality your product can naturally support
- Do not force virality where it does not exist — it will feel spammy and damage the brand
- If inherent or collaborative virality exists, your job is to reduce friction in the sharing moment
- If communicative virality exists, invest in the artifact that gets shared (make it beautiful, branded, useful)
- Incentivized virality works short-term but must be paired with genuine value
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Growth Quantitative Models">
Growth is not a feeling — it is math. Build quantitative models to project growth and make decisions.

**The growth equation:**
New Users = (Organic + Paid + Viral + Reactivated) - Churned Users

**Key model components:**
1. **Acquisition model**: By channel — volume × conversion rate × cost
2. **Activation model**: Signup → Onboarding steps → Core action (track drop-off at each step)
3. **Retention model**: Cohort curves by segment, time to flatten, steady-state retention rate
4. **Monetization model**: ARPU × Conversion to paid × Expansion revenue - Contraction revenue
5. **Viral model**: K-factor × cycle time (how fast the viral loop completes)

**For product marketing:**
- Build a simple model of your growth engine in a spreadsheet
- Identify the highest-leverage variable (the one where a small improvement has the biggest impact)
- Focus messaging and campaigns on that variable
- Use the model to set realistic goals and evaluate experiment impact
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="User Psychology and Motivation">
Understanding the hidden motivations behind user behavior is essential for product marketing:

**Motivation framework:**
1. **Functional motivation**: The user wants to complete a task more efficiently
2. **Emotional motivation**: The user wants to feel a certain way (confident, in control, creative)
3. **Social motivation**: The user wants to be perceived a certain way by others

**Trigger types:**
1. **External triggers**: Notifications, emails, ads — you control these
2. **Internal triggers**: Emotions, habits, routines — you must understand and design for these
3. **Contextual triggers**: Situations, environments, events — you must anticipate these

**Decision-making in product flows:**
- Users do not make rational decisions — they satisfice (good enough) and rely on heuristics
- Reduce cognitive load at every decision point
- Default options are powerful — most users will not change the default
- Loss aversion is stronger than gain motivation — frame value in terms of what they will lose without you

**For product marketing:**
- Messaging should address emotional and social motivations, not just functional
- Marketing triggers should align with the user's natural context (when do they feel the pain?)
- Positioning should leverage loss aversion: "Without [product], you are losing [specific thing]"
- Onboarding copy should reduce perceived effort and increase perceived reward at every step
</core_concept>
