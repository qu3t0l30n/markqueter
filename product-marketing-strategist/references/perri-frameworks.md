<core_concept name="The Build Trap">
The build trap is when organizations measure success by outputs (features shipped, story points completed) rather than outcomes (customer behavior changed, revenue impact, problem solved). Companies in the build trap treat their roadmap as a feature factory conveyor belt.

**Signals you are in the build trap:**
- Success is measured by "we shipped X features this quarter"
- Roadmaps are lists of features with dates, not problems to solve
- Product managers are project managers in disguise (managing timelines, not discovering value)
- Customer research happens once at the start, not continuously
- Marketing writes copy for features nobody asked for
- "The business" dictates what to build; teams just execute

**How to escape:**
- Reframe roadmaps around problems and outcomes, not solutions
- Give product teams autonomy to discover solutions
- Measure what matters: adoption, retention, revenue per user — not velocity
- Treat strategy as a framework for decision-making, not a feature list
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Product Kata">
An iterative framework for continuous product discovery, adapted from Toyota Kata:

**Step 1: Understand the direction**
- What is the strategic intent? What outcome are we trying to achieve?
- Align with company vision and product-level goals

**Step 2: Analyze the current state**
- Where are we now relative to the goal?
- What do the metrics tell us? What do customers tell us?
- How far is the gap between current state and desired outcome?

**Step 3: Identify the next target condition**
- What is the next measurable milestone toward the goal?
- This should be achievable in a short cycle (1-4 weeks)

**Step 4: Determine the biggest obstacle**
- What is the single biggest thing preventing us from reaching the target condition?
- Focus on one obstacle at a time

**Step 5: Experiment to remove the obstacle**
- Design the smallest experiment that teaches us whether we can overcome this obstacle
- Run it, measure it, learn from it
- Loop back to Step 2

**For product marketing, the Product Kata means:**
- Positioning is never "done" — it is continuously refined through market feedback
- Messaging should be tested with real users before committing to campaigns
- Go-to-market is iterative: launch → learn → adjust → re-launch
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Strategy Deployment">
Strategy is not a plan. It is a decision-making framework that connects vision to economic outcomes.

**The strategy stack:**
1. **Vision** — Where the company wants to be in 5-10 years
2. **Strategic intent** — The current business challenge or opportunity (1-3 year horizon)
3. **Product initiatives** — The problems to solve that support the strategic intent
4. **Options** — The possible solutions teams can explore for each initiative

**Key principles:**
- Each level provides constraints and direction for the level below
- Teams at the option level have autonomy to discover and validate solutions
- Strategy is communicated as interconnecting stories throughout the organization
- Everyone should be able to trace their work back to the strategic intent

**Deployment models:** OKRs (Google), Hoshin Kanri (Toyota), Mission Command (military) — all share the same premise: set direction at each level so teams can act autonomously.
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Solution Experimentation over MVP">
Perri redefines MVP as "the minimum amount of effort to learn." She prefers the term "solution experimentation" because:

- MVP has been corrupted to mean "crappy first version of the product"
- The goal is learning, not launching
- Experiments should be designed to test the riskiest assumption first

**Experiment types (ordered by effort):**
1. Problem interviews — Do people actually have this problem?
2. Solution interviews — Would this solution address the problem?
3. Concierge tests — Manually deliver the value to test willingness to use/pay
4. Wizard of Oz — Fake the backend, real frontend
5. Prototypes — Interactive but non-functional
6. Single-feature MVP — One thing, done well, instrumented for learning
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Outcomes Over Outputs for Marketing">
**Vanity metrics** (avoid):
- Page views, total signups, social followers, features shipped
- These always go up and tell you nothing about value creation

**Actionable metrics** (use):
- Activation rate (% of signups who reach "aha" moment)
- Retention cohorts (are users coming back?)
- Revenue per user (are we capturing value?)
- Time-to-value (how fast do users get value?)
- Net revenue retention (are existing customers expanding?)

**For product marketers, this means:**
- Campaign success is not impressions or clicks — it is pipeline generated and deals influenced
- Content success is not pageviews — it is activation and conversion
- Launch success is not PR mentions — it is adoption and retention in the first 30/60/90 days
</core_concept>

<core_concept name="Product Operations">
The emerging function that connects strategy, data, and process across product teams:

- **Data and insights**: Centralize customer research, analytics, and market intelligence so every team has access
- **Process and practices**: Standardize how teams do discovery, experimentation, and go-to-market
- **Tooling and infrastructure**: Ensure teams have the right tools and that knowledge is not siloed

**Why this matters for product marketing:**
Product marketing should be a core part of product operations — embedded in discovery, not bolted on after shipping. Product ops creates the feedback loops that make positioning and messaging evidence-based rather than opinion-based.
</core_concept>
