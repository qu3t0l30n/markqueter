<anti_patterns>

<objective>Recognize and avoid the most common product marketing mistakes. These anti-patterns are drawn from the collective wisdom of Perri, Cagan, Reforge, and Growth Rockstar. If you see these patterns, flag them immediately.</objective>

<anti_pattern name="The Feature Factory Marketing Machine">
**What it looks like:**
- Marketing exists to write release notes and feature announcements
- Every feature gets a blog post, regardless of strategic importance
- Product marketing is measured by "content shipped" not business impact
- The messaging is a list of features, not a narrative about transformation

**Why it fails:**
Customers do not buy features. They buy outcomes. A feature announcement without a problem-outcome narrative is noise.

**The fix:**
- Prioritize marketing investment by strategic importance, not feature count
- Every piece of content must answer: "What can the customer now do that they could not before?"
- Batch minor features into thematic narratives. Only flagship capabilities get standalone stories.
</anti_pattern>

<anti_pattern name="Positioning by Committee">
**What it looks like:**
- Positioning is decided in a room of executives arguing about adjectives
- Every stakeholder gets their favorite feature mentioned
- The positioning tries to be everything to everyone
- Competitive positioning is reactive ("they said X, so we should say Y")

**Why it fails:**
Positioning that tries to appeal to everyone appeals to no one. Differentiation requires sacrifice — choosing what you are NOT.

**The fix:**
- Positioning starts with customer discovery, not executive opinions
- One target segment, one primary differentiator, one core narrative
- Test positioning with real prospects before committing
- Review and update positioning quarterly based on market feedback
</anti_pattern>

<anti_pattern name="Launch and Forget">
**What it looks like:**
- Massive effort goes into the launch event (press, emails, social, webinars)
- After launch week, attention moves to the next launch
- No measurement of adoption, activation, or retention post-launch
- No iteration on messaging based on actual user behavior

**Why it fails:**
The launch is the beginning of learning, not the end of work. Most product adoption happens in the weeks and months after launch, not on launch day.

**The fix:**
- Plan for 30/60/90-day post-launch measurement and iteration
- Instrument the product to track feature adoption
- Build a feedback loop: usage data → messaging refinement → re-engagement
- Allocate marketing effort: 30% pre-launch, 70% post-launch
</anti_pattern>

<anti_pattern name="Acquisition Addiction">
**What it looks like:**
- All marketing budget goes to top-of-funnel acquisition
- Growth = more signups
- Retention is "the product team's problem"
- Churn is not measured or is ignored because new users mask it

**Why it fails:**
Pouring users into a leaky bucket is the most expensive way to grow. A 5% improvement in retention often has 10x the impact of a 5% improvement in acquisition.

**The fix:**
- Measure and report retention alongside acquisition in every marketing review
- Product marketing owns the activation and early retention narrative
- Lifecycle marketing investment should be proportional to retention's impact on revenue
- Challenge any growth plan that does not address retention
</anti_pattern>

<anti_pattern name="Strategy-Free Tactics">
**What it looks like:**
- "Let's try TikTok!" with no strategic rationale
- Channel selection based on trends, not Four Fits analysis
- Campaign ideas generated without connecting to product strategy
- Marketing OKRs that do not trace back to business outcomes

**Why it fails:**
Tactics without strategy is just random activity. Every channel and campaign should be traceable to a strategic objective.

**The fix:**
- Before any tactic, answer: "Which fit does this address? What outcome does this drive?"
- Kill any campaign that cannot be connected to the product strategy stack
- Use the Four Fits framework to evaluate channel viability before investing
- Set OKRs that connect marketing activity to business outcomes, not vanity metrics
</anti_pattern>

<anti_pattern name="The Internal Echo Chamber">
**What it looks like:**
- Messaging is written by marketers for marketers
- Positioning is validated by internal reviews, not customer feedback
- Win/loss analysis is not conducted or is based on CRM data only (no customer interviews)
- The competitive landscape is based on what sales "feels" not what customers say

**Why it fails:**
Your internal team is the worst judge of your messaging. They are too close, too biased, and too invested.

**The fix:**
- Every major messaging decision must include external validation (customer interviews, A/B tests, message testing)
- Conduct win/loss interviews with actual prospects (not just the sales team's interpretation)
- Build a customer advisory board for ongoing messaging feedback
- Use discovery techniques (Cagan) to continuously validate positioning
</anti_pattern>

<anti_pattern name="The TAM Fantasy">
**What it looks like:**
- "The global market for X is $500B, if we capture just 1%..."
- Addressable market is calculated top-down from industry reports
- No distinction between total market, serviceable market, and obtainable market
- Market sizing is used to justify strategy, not to inform it

**Why it fails:**
Model-Market Fit requires realistic math. A $500B TAM is meaningless if your realistic addressable segment is $10M.

**The fix:**
- Size the market bottom-up: How many companies match your ICP? What is realistic ARPU? What is realistic market share?
- Distinguish between TAM, SAM, and SOM with honest assumptions
- Validate market size with actual sales data, not analyst reports
- Use market sizing to inform strategy: which segments to target first, how to sequence expansion
</anti_pattern>

</anti_patterns>
