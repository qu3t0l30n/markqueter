<product_narrative_workflow>

<objective>Craft a compelling product narrative rooted in customer outcomes, not features. The narrative is the strategic story that unifies all marketing communication — from the homepage headline to the sales pitch to the investor deck.</objective>

<required_reading>
- references/perri-frameworks.md (Strategy Deployment, Outcomes Over Outputs, Build Trap)
- references/cagan-principles.md (Product Vision, Product Strategy, Four Risks)
- references/growth-rockstar-insights.md (User Psychology, Motivation Framework)
</required_reading>

<process>

**Phase 1: The transformation arc**

Every great product narrative is a transformation story. Map the arc:

```
BEFORE STATE: [What the customer's world looks like without your product]
├── Frustrations: [What irritates them daily]
├── Limitations: [What they cannot do]
├── Risks: [What they fear]
└── Workarounds: [What they cobble together today]

TRIGGER: [The moment they realize the status quo is unacceptable]

AFTER STATE: [What the customer's world looks like with your product]
├── Capabilities: [What they can now do]
├── Outcomes: [What results they achieve]
├── Confidence: [How they feel]
└── Identity: [Who they become]
```

Ask the user:
- "Describe your best customer's life BEFORE they found you — what was frustrating, broken, or limiting?"
- "What was the trigger moment — what made them start looking for a solution?"
- "Describe their life AFTER using your product for 3-6 months — what changed?"

If the user cannot describe this arc from real customer experience, recommend conducting customer interviews first (Perri's Product Kata, Step 2: Analyze current state).

**Phase 2: The narrative spine**

Build the core narrative structure:

**1. The world has changed.**
Open with the market shift, industry trend, or emerging reality that makes the old way unsustainable. This is not about your product — it is about the world your customer lives in.

**2. The old way no longer works.**
Describe the pain of the status quo. Use specific, vivid language drawn from real customer experiences. Make the audience feel the problem.

**3. There is a better way.**
Introduce your approach (not your product yet — your approach, philosophy, or principle). This is the conceptual shift that makes your solution possible.

**4. Here is how it works.**
Now introduce the product as the embodiment of that better way. Connect features to the transformation they enable.

**5. Here is the proof.**
Provide evidence: customer results, metrics, testimonials, case studies. Make the transformation concrete and credible.

**6. Here is your next step.**
Close with a clear, low-friction path forward. Reduce risk ("free trial", "no commitment") and create urgency ("join 500 teams already using this").

**Phase 3: Audience adaptation**

The same narrative spine adapts to different audiences. For each key audience, adjust:

| Element | Executive buyer | Technical evaluator | End user | Investor/analyst |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|----------|-----------------|
| "World changed" | Market/competitive pressure | Technology shift | Workflow evolution | Market opportunity |
| "Old way broken" | Revenue/efficiency loss | Technical debt/limitation | Daily frustration | Incumbent weakness |
| "Better way" | Strategic advantage | Architecture/approach | Simplicity/power | Category creation |
| "How it works" | Business outcomes | Technical capabilities | Feature experience | Product differentiation |
| "Proof" | ROI, case studies | Benchmarks, integrations | Peer reviews, demos | Growth metrics, market data |
| "Next step" | Executive briefing | Technical evaluation | Free trial | Analyst briefing |

**Phase 4: Narrative consistency check**

Ensure the narrative is consistent with strategic frameworks:

**Perri check — Is this outcome-driven?**
- Does the narrative lead with a customer outcome or a product feature?
- Would Melissa Perri say this is about value creation or about shipping?
- Fix: Rewrite any feature-first language into outcome-first language

**Cagan check — Is this discovery-informed?**
- Is the narrative based on validated customer insights or internal assumptions?
- Has the core claim (transformation) been tested with real prospects?
- Fix: Flag any unvalidated claims and recommend discovery experiments

**Reforge check — Does this fit the strategy stack?**
- Does the narrative connect to the product strategy (not just the product)?
- Is it appropriate for the current strategic phase?
- Fix: Align the narrative to the current strategy focus, not the entire vision

**Growth Rockstar check — Does this address motivation?**
- Does the narrative address functional, emotional, AND social motivations?
- Does it leverage the right triggers (external, internal, contextual)?
- Fix: Add emotional and social dimensions if the narrative is purely functional

**Phase 5: Format the narrative**

Produce the narrative in multiple formats:

1. **The elevator pitch** (30 seconds / 50 words): The transformation in one breath
2. **The executive summary** (2 minutes / 200 words): The full arc, compressed
3. **The homepage narrative** (visual layout with headlines for each section of the spine)
4. **The sales deck narrative** (slide-by-slide structure following the narrative spine)
5. **The investor narrative** (market opportunity → problem → solution → traction → vision)

Each format should tell the same story at different depths. A prospect who reads only the elevator pitch should understand the core transformation.

**Phase 6: Narrative testing plan**

The narrative is a hypothesis. Validate it:

1. **Internal alignment test**: Present to sales, CS, and product. Do they recognize the story from customer interactions?
2. **Prospect reaction test**: Share with 5-10 target prospects. Does it resonate? Does it match their experience?
3. **A/B test**: Test the narrative against the current messaging on a landing page or email. Measure engagement.
4. **Sales call test**: Have sales use the new narrative in 10 calls. Track conversion vs. baseline.

</process>

<success_criteria>
- Transformation arc is grounded in real customer experience
- Narrative spine follows all six elements
- Audience adaptations maintain consistency while speaking to different concerns
- All four framework checks pass
- Narrative is delivered in at least 3 formats (elevator pitch, executive summary, and one more)
- Validation plan is concrete
- No feature-first language — every feature connects to an outcome
</success_criteria>
